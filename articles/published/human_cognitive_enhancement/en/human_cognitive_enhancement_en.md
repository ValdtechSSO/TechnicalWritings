# Will cognitive enhancement be humanity's next great challenge?

Lately I've been deep into studying the human brain, memory, and neuroscience. And the more I read, the more convinced I become of one idea: one of humanity's great goals over the next 50 to 100 years should be cognitive enhancement.

Not just because it would let us keep expanding our capabilities in an era dominated by artificial intelligence, but because it would help us unlock the full potential of that technology and accelerate scientific progress like never before.

Imagine for a moment that we could have a fully biological brain, but operating near the limits that nature itself has already shown to be possible. The advantages could be extraordinary: near-perfect memory, much faster learning, greater reasoning capacity, more creativity, and better control over our own bodies and cognitive processes.

And no, I'm not talking about transhumanism based on brain implants. Personally, the idea of carrying a chip wired into my brain doesn't appeal to me. Beyond how invasive such an intervention would be, it raises serious questions about security, privacy, and technological dependence.

In my view, if we ever achieve true cognitive enhancement, it will come by a completely different route: a combination of precision gene editing, precision pharmacology, molecular biology, cell therapies, and other technologies we're only just beginning to develop.

Of course, the challenge is enormous.

The brain is probably the most complex biological system we know of. Although it makes up around 2% of body weight, it consumes close to 20% of the body's total energy. And any attempt to improve it would have to respect an extremely delicate balance between energy consumption, neural plasticity, cellular repair, waste clearance, oxidative stress, sleep quality, and many other processes we still only partly understand.

Because the brain didn't evolve to maximize a single capability. It's the result of millions of years searching for a balance between intelligence, survival, reproduction, energy efficiency, and stability.

And this is where, during a conversation on the topic, a question came up that I can't get out of my head:

**What if nature has already found many of the solutions we're looking for?**

There are people with truly extraordinary abilities. Individuals with exceptional memory, an unusual capacity for learning, a reduced need for sleep, or even mutations that protect them against certain neurodegenerative diseases. Evolution has spent millions of years exploring genetic variants. So why not study those variants and try to understand the biological mechanisms that make them possible?

Imagine a global database with millions of genomes and functional studies that could identify variants associated with especially beneficial traits: one that improves memory, another that reduces the need for sleep, another that protects against neural decline, another that improves the energy efficiency of neurons, another that reduces brain inflammation.

That said, I'm fully aware that this is where the hard part lies. Biology rarely hands out capabilities for free. Many of these variants likely come with trade-offs: a memory that never forgets can make it harder to move on, and sleeping less may carry a cost we don't yet know how to measure. My own premise—that the brain seeks balance rather than maximizing a single capability—suggests precisely that it won't be enough to simply "stack" beneficial variants as if they had no downsides. There probably isn't a single "intelligence gene" either, but thousands of genes interacting with each other in ways we still don't understand.

And even so, I believe we're entering a fascinating era. The combination of artificial intelligence, genomics, computational biology, and neuroscience may allow us to build increasingly accurate predictive models of how cells and the brain work.

Perhaps the future isn't about inventing entirely new capabilities, but about understanding and reproducing biological mechanisms that already exist in some individuals and that evolution itself has shown to be possible.

I don't know whether we'll live to see true cognitive enhancement. What I do believe is that we're starting to walk the path that could make it real. And if I had to bet on one of the great scientific projects of this century, I'd bet on exactly this one.

What do you think? Will the future of human intelligence run through biology, or through integration with technology?
